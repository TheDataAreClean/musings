Speed is not neutral. Every tool has a native pace and moving at a different pace is uncomfortable — faster than the tool allows is frustrating, slower than it encourages is strange.

Writing tools have become fast. The text appears immediately. The document saves constantly. The suggestion arrives before you finish the sentence. There is no lag, no friction, no moment of resistance.

I have started to miss resistance.

## The Information in Friction

A typewriter resists. The key must be pressed firmly. The carriage must be returned. The correction is laborious — white fluid, dried, typed over, always slightly visible. The machine does not encourage you to change your mind.

This sounds like a disadvantage. In some ways it is. But the resistance is also information. When revision is costly, you think before you type. The friction is a signal: *is this actually what I want to say?*

The frictionless tool removes that signal. Revision is so cheap that you can defer the thinking. Type something, change it, change it back, change it to something else. The document becomes a space where the thinking happens, rather than where it lands.

This is sometimes useful. It is also a way of avoiding the harder work of deciding.

{% callout "warning" %}
I am not recommending typewriters. The observation is about friction as signal, not about the specific implementation. A longer feedback loop — writing by hand first, typing second — can provide the same thing with fewer mechanical complications.
{% endcallout %}

## The Pace of Understanding

Reading also has a speed. The eye can move faster than comprehension. Speed-reading techniques, taken to an extreme, produce a person who has moved their eyes over every word and understood very little.

The useful pace for reading is slower than the comfortable pace. This is not a comfortable observation.

What slows reading down profitably:

1. Pausing after paragraphs to ask what was just said
2. Reading aloud, which forces you to the pace of speech
3. Note-taking, which requires you to produce a translation
4. Re-reading, which is the admission that the first pass was insufficient

None of these are efficient. All of them improve comprehension.

## A Table of Speeds

| Activity | Fast version | Slow version | What you lose going fast |
|---|---|---|---|
| Writing | Stream of consciousness | Draft, revise, rest, revise | The knowledge of what you mean |
| Reading | Skim | Read, pause, re-read | The structure of the argument |
| Deciding | Gut reaction | Sleep on it | The input of your future self |
| Making | Ship fast | Finish properly | The details that earn trust |

The fast versions are not wrong. Sometimes they are correct. The point is that the fast version is a choice with costs, not simply the better version of the same thing.

## The Wrong Speed

There is a particular kind of clarity that comes from doing something at the wrong speed — slower than you are expected to, slower than the tool encourages, slower than would be efficient.

The wrong speed forces you to see the work. You notice things you would have moved past. You have time to be surprised by what you are making.

I write slowly. I have always written slowly. For a long time I thought this was a deficiency. I have started to think it is the thing I am most careful to protect.
