There is a certain kind of web page that announces itself before you read it. The font is a bespoke variable type. The scroll triggers animations. The margins are deliberate in a way that wants you to notice they are deliberate.

I find I don't trust these pages.

## The Document as Default

Before the web had an aesthetic, it had a default. That default was: a page. Text, links, headings. The browser's own interpretation of semantic structure. It was legible. It was fast. It was, in the word everyone uses to damn it, *plain*.

Plainness is not the absence of design. It is the discipline to design only what needs designing.

A document editor doesn't fight you. It gets out of the way of the words. The chrome is there — the ruler, the margins, the page number at the bottom — but it's *system* chrome, not *author* chrome. It doesn't say anything about taste. It says: here is where writing goes.

## What Gets Lost in the Making of Things Look Good

There is a cost to every pixel of decoration. The cost is not always visual — it's cognitive. Every element on a page that is not content is asking the reader to process something that isn't the reason they came.

The decorative border. The hover state on a card. The fade-in on scroll. These are all questions. The reader has to answer: *is this meaningful? is this interactive? should I attend to this?* Usually the answer is no, and the reader moves on. But the question was still asked. There are hundreds of these questions on a well-designed contemporary website.

A document asks one question: *what do you want to read?*

## Brutalism as Honesty

Brutalist architecture was misnamed from the start. The *brut* is *béton brut* — raw concrete, exposed. It is honesty about material, not brutality in any human sense.

Brutalist web design is similar. It says: here is HTML. Here are the links. Here is the text. I have not hidden these things inside a component system.

This is not a rejection of craft. It is a different kind of craft — the craft of the woodworker who lets the grain show rather than painting over it.

The grain here is the browser. The document model. The default stylesheet. These are not bugs to be fixed. They are the material.

## A Note on Fonts

I am writing this in a monospace font. Not because I am a programmer (though I am) but because monospace fonts are document fonts. They emerged from typewriters, from terminals, from the act of *putting words somewhere physical*. They are fonts that have work to do.

The font does not perform warmth. It does not perform cleverness. It performs legibility.

That is enough.

---

The most direct thing is usually the right thing. This is true in writing, in code, and in the design of the surfaces where writing and code live.

This page is an attempt to live by that principle. I don't know if it succeeds. But it is trying, without decoration, to try.[^1]

[^1]: There is a version of this argument that becomes precious — the austerity that wants you to notice the austerity. I am aware of this failure mode. I am trying to avoid it. Whether I succeed is for you to say.
